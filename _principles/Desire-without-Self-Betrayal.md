<p class="principle-progress">Principle 7 of 7</p>

<p><em>A grounded orientation to desire.</em></p>

<p> For a long time, I assumed desire itself was the problem. </p>

<p> It felt safer to want less than to risk disappointment, rejection, or discovering that what I longed for would never be returned. </p>

<p> Over time, I realised something quieter. The problem wasn't desire. It was everything I learned to do in order to hide it. </p>

<p> What causes pain is not wanting. It's how desire becomes distorted when love, safety, or approval feel uncertain. </p>

<p> When wanting feels risky, desire often reshapes itself into: </p>

<ul> <li>caretaking</li> <li>over-responsibility</li> <li>self-silencing</li> <li>waiting to be chosen</li> <li>earning closeness through effort</li> </ul>

<p> This principle is about learning to want without disappearing. </p>

<h3>1. Wanting Is Not Immaturity</h3>

<p> For a long time, wanting felt unsafe. </p>

<p> Wanting carried the risk of disappointment, shame, or learning that my needs were too much, too inconvenient, or unwelcome. </p>

<p> So I adapted. </p>

<ul> <li>I softened desire.</li> <li>I rationalised it.</li> <li>I turned it into usefulness instead.</li> </ul>

<p> This wasn't weakness. </p>

<p> It was a strategy shaped in conditions where wanting had consequences. </p>

<p> Desire itself was never the danger. </p>

<h3>2. Desire Becomes Distorted When It Is Unnamed</h3>

<p> Unacknowledged desire rarely disappears. </p>

<p> Instead, it tends to leak out as: </p>

<ul> <li>over-giving</li> <li>staying too long</li> <li>hoping without clarity</li> <li>resentment</li> <li>confusion about your own needs</li> </ul>

<p> Naming what you want does not obligate anyone to meet it. </p>

<p> It simply restores honesty with yourself. </p>

<p> Clarity reduces self-betrayal. </p>

<h3>3. Love Is Not Secured Through Rescue</h3>

<p> For me, desire once became entangled with responsibility. </p>

<p> Love became something I tried to maintain through: </p>

<ul> <li>holding things together</li> <li>anticipating needs</li> <li>staying steady for others</li> </ul>

<p> This principle draws a line. </p>

<p> Desire does not require: </p>

<ul> <li>rescuing</li> <li>managing</li> <li>carrying emotional weight for another adult</li> </ul>

<p> If wanting requires self-erasure, it is no longer love. </p>

<h3>4. Desire Can Collapse Under Authority</h3>

<p> When someone holds power — to evaluate, approve, or withhold — desire can quietly shift. </p>

<p> Wanting becomes: </p>

<ul> <li>being seen correctly</li> <li>being chosen</li> <li>being approved of</li> </ul>

<p> This isn't pathology. </p>

<p> It's a nervous system response shaped by earlier experiences where power and safety became linked. </p>

<p> This principle isn't about eliminating that response. </p>

<p> It's about noticing it — and staying with yourself instead of adapting to secure closeness. </p>

<h3>5. Wanting Does Not Make You Unsafe</h3>

<p> At the centre of this principle is a quiet fear: </p>

<ul> <li>if I want clearly, I will lose connection</li> <li>if I stop adapting, I will be left</li> <li>if I choose myself, love will disappear</li> </ul>

<p> This principle gently questions those beliefs. </p>

<p> Wanting does not make you selfish. </p>

<p> It makes you honest. </p>

<h3>The Orientation: Want Clearly. Stay Present. Do Not Disappear to Be Loved.</h3>

<p> Ask yourself: </p>

<ul> <li>Am I naming what I want — or hiding it?</li> <li>Am I adapting to be chosen — or choosing consciously?</li> <li>Does this desire expand me — or shrink me?</li> <li>Am I staying present with myself — or negotiating my worth?</li> </ul>

<p> Desire is not a demand. </p>

<p> It is information. </p>

<p><strong>That is desire without self-betrayal.</strong></p>

<h3>A Final Thought</h3>

<p> For a long time, I believed the safest way to keep connection was to become easier to love. </p>

<p> To ask for less. Need less. Want less. Adapt a little more. Disappear a little more. </p>

<p> For a while, that seemed to work. </p>

<p> But every relationship built on self-abandonment quietly asks you to keep paying the same price. </p>

<p> This principle isn't about getting everything you want. </p>

<p> It's about no longer believing that being loved requires leaving yourself behind. </p>

<p> You can want honestly. </p>

<p> You can hear "no." </p>

<p> You can survive disappointment. </p>

<p> And you can remain fully yourself throughout all of it. </p>

<p> I don't know if that's the end of the work. I suspect it isn't. But it feels much closer to the person I'm still becoming. </p>
