Lately I've become aware of a kind of grief I don't think I've ever properly named.

It isn't the grief of losing another person.

Or losing a relationship.

Or losing a particular future.

It's quieter than that.

It's the grief of slowly realising that I cannot become everything I once believed I needed to be.

---

## The Fantasy Of Becoming Enough

For years I carried an unspoken belief.

If I learned enough...

Worked hard enough...

Healed enough...

Achieved enough...

Eventually I would arrive somewhere.

A version of myself that no longer felt uncertain.

No longer felt inadequate.

No longer worried about being left behind.

Looking back, I don't think I was chasing success.

I was chasing safety.

---

## What My Body Already Knew

The strange thing is that my body seemed to understand the truth long before my mind did.

Whenever I tried to carry too much, something happened.

My head would begin to pound.

Everything became irritating.

Noise felt louder.

People felt harder to be around.

Even small decisions became exhausting.

In those moments I didn't feel like an adult managing a busy life.

I felt like a child.

Small.

Overwhelmed.

Certain that I couldn't cope with what was in front of me.

For a long time I interpreted that as failure.

Now I wonder whether it was information.

---

## Trying To Be Everything

I don't think I was overwhelmed because I had too much to do.

I think I was overwhelmed because I was trying to become too much.

Capable enough.

Productive enough.

Emotionally healed enough.

Financially secure enough.

Useful enough.

As though becoming enough in every direction might finally earn me permission to rest.

That permission never came.

Because there was always another version of myself waiting to be built.

---

## The Two Lives

Sometimes it feels as though there are two versions of my life pulling in opposite directions.

One wants something beautifully ordinary.

Meaningful work.

Time with Victor.

Walking the dog.

Writing.

Counselling.

Conversations that matter.

The other still believes my worth depends on becoming extraordinary.

Achieving more.

Doing more.

Being more.

Whenever that second voice takes over, I eventually pay for it.

Not emotionally.

Physically.

My body withdraws its cooperation.

As though it's saying,

> *"I can't carry this version of your life anymore."*

---

## The Grief

Perhaps this is why it feels like grief.

Because something really is being lost.

Not my ambition.

Not my curiosity.

Not my desire to contribute.

What's disappearing is the fantasy that one day I will finally become enough to deserve peace.

That fantasy accompanied me for a long time.

In some ways it protected me.

It gave me hope that relief was just beyond the next achievement.

Letting go of it feels strangely sad.

Even though it is also freeing.

---

## What Acceptance Isn't

Acceptance doesn't mean lowering my standards.

Or giving up on growth.

Or deciding that none of it matters.

It simply means accepting that I will always be a limited human being.

I will never read every book.

Master every skill.

Help every person.

Become every possible version of myself.

There are lives I will never live.

Paths I will never take.

People I will never become.

There is grief in that.

But there is also clarity.

Because every life becomes real only when countless other lives are allowed to fall away.

---

## What I'm Beginning To Choose

The more I accept my limits, the more gently I seem to treat myself.

Rest no longer feels like failure.

Saying no feels less like missing out.

Ordinary days begin to feel sufficient.

Not because I've stopped growing.

Because I've stopped demanding that growth finally make me worthy.

---

## Living As Someone Finite

I'm beginning to think maturity isn't becoming limitless.

It's learning how to live peacefully inside our limits.

Not every opportunity is mine.

Not every ambition needs to be pursued.

Not every part of me needs to be perfected.

Perhaps the deepest freedom isn't becoming everything I could be.

Perhaps it's finally grieving the fact that I never could.

And discovering that I was enough to begin living long before I believed I was.
